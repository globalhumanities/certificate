Contested Narratives 

 

This course explores a variety of human records,  i.e. cultural, artistic, and intellectual traditions across geographic, temporal, and conceptual borders, fostering a deeper global understanding of the human experience on a global scale. It deploys approaches from the humanities to familiarize students with a plurality of global voices, distinct cultural artefacts, and modes of thought and practice. It enables students to apply different disciplinary approaches to examine and interrogate global challenges. 

The ability to tell stories is one of the fundamental things that make us human. But the narratives that we tell about ourselves, others, and the world feature different ways of perceiving, experiencing, and being in that very world - or the plurality of worlds we can imagine. More broadly, these competing narratives also pose questions about the relationship between history and memory, fact and fiction, subjective perception and objective reality, authorship and audience, intent and reception, falsification and verifiability - all of which might be approached from the range of humanistic disciplines. Through intensive and engaged analysis of various artifacts across diverse cultural, geographic, and historical contexts, the course seeks to develop students’ awareness of the variety of approaches to constructing and contesting narratives in the global world.
